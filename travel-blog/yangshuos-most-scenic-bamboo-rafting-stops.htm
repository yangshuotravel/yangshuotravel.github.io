Yangshuo’s Most Scenic Bamboo Rafting Stops

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

There’s a certain magic to Yangshuo that transcends the typical travel experience. It’s a place where limestone karsts pierce the sky like dragon’s teeth, where the Li River meanders with the grace of a silk ribbon, and where time seems to slow down to the gentle pace of a bamboo raft gliding on emerald water. While the town itself buzzes with energy, cafes, and the chatter of global adventurers, the true soul of Yangshuo is found on the water. Bamboo rafting isn’t just an activity here; it’s a pilgrimage into the very heart of a landscape that has inspired poets and painters for centuries. This is your guide to the most scenic stops along this unforgettable journey, blending natural wonder with the vibrant culture that makes this region a perpetual hotspot.

The Timeless Allure of the Bamboo Raft

Before we delve into the specific stops, it’s essential to understand the vessel itself. The modern "bamboo raft" is often made from PVC pipes for safety and durability, cleverly designed to resemble traditional bamboo. They are poled by skilled local operators, many of whom have stories about these rivers that span generations. This practice is a cornerstone of the local economy and a beautiful example of sustainable tourism when done responsibly. The gentle, silent propulsion (save for the dip of the pole and the whisper of the water) is what sets this experience apart from motorized boats. It allows for an immersive, almost meditative connection with the environment, making you an observer in a living painting.

The Crown Jewel: The Li River from Xingping to Nine-Horse Fresco Hill

While several sections offer rafting, the stretch from the ancient town of Xingping is arguably the most iconic. This is the landscape featured on the back of the 20 RMB note, and for good reason.

Xingping Old Town: The Gateway

Your journey begins in Xingping, a town with a history stretching back over 1,700 years. Unlike the more developed Yangshuo town, Xingping retains a rustic, ancient charm with its old streets and Ming and Qing dynasty architecture. A stop here isn't just about getting on the raft; it's about stepping back in time. The town has become a hotspot for photographers and culture seekers, with small guesthouses and traditional restaurants nestled among the old buildings. It’s the perfect prelude to the natural spectacle ahead.

The 20 RMB Viewpoint: A Photo Op for the Ages

Just a short distance from the launch point, your rafter will almost certainly point out the exact spot to capture the view that mirrors the famous banknote. Have your 20 RMB ready! This stop is more than a photo opportunity; it’s a moment of cultural recognition. It’s where every traveler gets to hold up a piece of currency and align it with the breathtaking reality—the jagged peaks, the serene river, the lush foliage. It’s a quintessential Yangshuo experience and a powerful reminder of how this landscape is deeply woven into China’s identity.

Nine-Horse Fresco Hill (Jiuma Huashan)

As you continue downriver, you’ll be greeted by a massive cliff face that seems to tell a story. This is the Nine-Horse Fresco Hill. Legend says that the number of horses you can identify in the rock formations determines your intelligence and fortune. Spotting all nine is a feat few can claim! Your rafter will likely slow down, giving you ample time to stare, squint, and let your imagination run wild. This interactive stop engages you directly with the folklore of the region, turning a geological formation into a playful, memorable challenge.

The Yulong River: Yangshuo’s Serene Alternative

For those seeking a slightly more peaceful and intimate experience, the Yulong River is a must. A tributary of the Li, the Yulong is narrower, shallower, and often less crowded. It’s known as the "Little Li River" and is famed for its crystal-clear waters and idyllic countryside views. The rafting here often includes going over small, manual dams, which adds a fun, slight thrill to the tranquil ride.

Fuli Bridge: The Starting Point

Many Yulong River journeys begin near Fuli Bridge, an ancient stone bridge that adds a photogenic historical element to the landscape. The area around Fuli is also famous for its painted fans, a traditional craft that has become a popular souvenir. This cultural hotspot connects the activity of rafting with local artistry, a trending topic among travelers who seek authentic, hands-on cultural experiences.

The Ancient Dams: Nature’s Gentle Rollercoasters

A unique feature of the Yulong River rafting are the small, concrete dams you traverse. As your raft approaches the drop, the front tips down and splashes into the pool below. It’s a moment of joyful excitement that usually results in laughter and, on a hot day, a welcome cooling spray. It’s a perfect example of how the experience blends utter tranquility with little bursts of adventure.

Riverside Countryside: A Glimpse into Local Life

Without the large commercial ships of the Li River, the Yulong offers an unfiltered view of rural Guangxi life. You’ll drift past water buffalo cooling off in the river, farmers working in fields of vibrant green, and local children playing on the banks. This stop isn’t a formal one, but a continuous, moving panorama that is arguably the most scenic part of the trip. It appeals to the growing trend of "slow travel," where the journey itself is the destination.

Beyond the Raft: Integrating Hotspots and Responsible Travel

A blog about Yangshuo’s scenic stops wouldn’t be complete without addressing the context around this popular activity.

The Electric Bike Connection: A Perfect Pairing

One of the biggest travel trends in Yangshuo is renting an electric bike to explore the countryside. The beauty of the Yulong River area is that you can combine both experiences. A popular itinerary is to bike upriver, taking in the sights and sounds of the villages, and then take a one-way raft trip back down. This combo adventure is a huge hotspot with independent travelers as it offers autonomy, exploration, and relaxation all in one day.

Sunrise and Sunset: Capturing the Magic Light

The golden hours transform the karst mountains into a dazzling display of light and shadow. While not a specific "stop" on the rafting route, timing your trip for early morning or late afternoon is a pro-tip for photographers and romantics alike. The way the low sun illuminates the peaks and reflects off the water is nothing short of magical. This pursuit of the perfect shot is a key driver of tourism and social media content, making times like these incredibly popular.

Respecting the Environment: The Ultimate Hot Topic

As with any major tourist destination, the impact of visitors is a crucial conversation. The shift from genuine bamboo to PVC rafts was partly an environmental decision to preserve bamboo forests. However, the issue of litter remains. The most important "stop" any traveler can make is a mental one—to commit to being responsible. Pack out what you pack in, choose operators who follow eco-friendly practices, and understand that preserving this beauty is a shared responsibility. Sustainable travel is not just a buzzword; it’s the only way to ensure these scenic stops remain pristine for generations to come.

The bamboo rafting stops of Yangshuo offer more than just pretty views. They offer a pathway into a legendary landscape, a connection to ancient culture, and a moment of profound peace. From the iconic 20 RMB view to the gentle hum of rural life along the Yulong, each stop is a chapter in a story you’ll be eager to tell long after you’ve returned home.
