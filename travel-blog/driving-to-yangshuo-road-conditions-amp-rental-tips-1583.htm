Driving to Yangshuo: Road Conditions & Rental Tips

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

Yangshuo, with its iconic karst mountains and serene Li River, is a dream destination for travelers seeking both adventure and relaxation. While many visitors arrive via Guilin, driving to Yangshuo offers unparalleled freedom to explore hidden gems at your own pace. Whether you're renting a car or bringing your own, understanding road conditions, rental logistics, and scenic routes is key to a smooth journey.

Road Conditions: What to Expect

Highways from Guilin to Yangshuo

The most common starting point for a drive to Yangshuo is Guilin, roughly 65 kilometers (40 miles) away. The Guilin-Yangshuo Highway (G65) is the fastest route, taking about 1.5 hours. This well-maintained expressway offers smooth driving with minimal traffic, though tolls apply (around ¥30-50).

For a more scenic (but slower) alternative, take Route 321, which winds through rural villages and limestone peaks. This two-lane road is narrower and occasionally congested with local traffic, but the views are worth the extra time.

Rural Roads & Countryside Driving

Once in Yangshuo, smaller roads like those leading to Xingping or Yulong River are paved but can be narrow. Watch for scooters, bicycles, and occasional livestock. GPS apps like Gaode Maps (in Chinese) or Google Maps (limited offline functionality) are helpful, but download offline maps in advance.

Weather Considerations

Rainy seasons (April–June) may cause minor flooding or slippery roads, especially in rural areas. Fog is common in winter, reducing visibility on mountain routes. Always check forecasts before heading out.

Renting a Car: Tips & Pitfalls

Where to Rent

Most travelers rent cars in Guilin, as Yangshuo has limited rental options. International chains like Hertz or Avis operate in Guilin, but local agencies (e.g., eHi Car Rental) often offer better rates. Book online in advance, especially during peak seasons (October holidays, summer).

Required Documents

  • Valid driver’s license: An International Driving Permit (IDP) is technically required but rarely checked. A Chinese translation of your license may suffice.
  • Passport & credit card: Mandatory for deposits (usually ¥3,000–5,000).
  • Insurance: Opt for full coverage—local driving habits can be unpredictable.

Choosing the Right Vehicle

  • Compact cars (e.g., Volkswagen Polo) are ideal for solo travelers or couples navigating narrow roads.
  • SUVs provide better clearance for rural detours but are harder to park in Yangshuo’s tight spaces.
  • Electric vehicles (EVs): Charging stations are scarce outside Guilin; stick to gas-powered cars for now.

Navigation & Traffic Rules

  • Drive on the right, and expect occasional scooters swerving into your lane.
  • Speed limits: 120 km/h on highways, 40–60 km/h in towns. Speed cameras are common.
  • Parking: Yangshuo’s town center has paid lots (¥10–20/hour). Rural attractions often offer free but unmarked parking.

Scenic Detours Worth the Drive

1. Xingping Ancient Town

A 45-minute drive from Yangshuo, Xingping is quieter and boasts the 20 RMB note viewpoint. Park at the town entrance and wander the cobblestone streets.

2. Yulong River Bamboo Rafting

Skip the crowded Li River and drive 30 minutes to Gongnong Bridge, where private rafting tours start. Leave your car at nearby lots (¥10/day).

3. Moon Hill & Silver Cave

These attractions have ample parking. Arrive early to avoid tour buses—Moon Hill’s staircase hike rewards with panoramic views.

Alternative Transport: When Not to Drive

  • Electric bikes: Rent one in Yangshuo (¥50/day) for short trips. No license needed!
  • Private drivers: Hire a car with a driver (¥400–600/day) if you’re uncomfortable navigating.

Final Checks Before Hitting the Road

  • Fuel up: Gas stations are sparse near Yangshuo; fill up in Guilin.
  • Emergency numbers: Dial 122 for traffic accidents, 120 for medical help.
  • Cash: Some rural tolls or parking spots don’t accept mobile payments.

Driving to Yangshuo unlocks a side of Guangxi most tourists miss—just plan ahead, stay flexible, and keep your camera ready for those mountain curves.
